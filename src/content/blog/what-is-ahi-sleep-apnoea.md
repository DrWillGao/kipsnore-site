If you have seen "AHI" on a result, a leaflet or a guide and wondered what is AHI in plain terms, this page explains it. It is a clinical measure of sleep apnoea, and the bands tell you how severe a case is.

## What is AHI and what does it stand for?

AHI stands for apnoea-hypopnoea index. It counts two kinds of breathing event per hour of sleep:

- An **apnoea** is a complete pause in breathing lasting 10 seconds or more.
- A **hypopnoea** is a partial reduction in breathing, also lasting 10 seconds or more.

Add both together, divide by the hours you slept, and you get your AHI. It comes from a sleep study that measures your breathing, oxygen and other signals through the night, not from a microphone listening to snoring.

## What are the AHI severity bands?

The NHS and NICE use the same thresholds to grade obstructive sleep apnoea. Below 5 is considered normal.

| AHI (events per hour) | Severity |
| --- | --- |
| Under 5 | Normal |
| 5 to 14 | Mild |
| 15 to 30 | Moderate |
| Over 30 | Severe |

These are clinical bands. They are set and read by doctors, not by consumers or apps.

## What about ODI and RDI?

You may see two related measures on a report:

- **ODI** (oxygen desaturation index) counts how many times your blood oxygen dips per hour. It is separate from AHI.
- **RDI** (respiratory disturbance index) adds breathing-effort arousals to the apnoeas and hypopnoeas, so it usually reads a little higher than AHI.

All three come from monitored sleep studies. In the UK, that usually starts with a home test (respiratory polygraphy), with a fuller in-lab study for unclear cases.

## Can an app give me an AHI?

No. AHI requires measuring airflow and oxygen, which a phone cannot do from sound alone. Loud snoring tends to rise with apnoea severity, but it cannot confirm or rule it out. An app cannot diagnose sleep apnoea, and only a clinical sleep study can.

What a tracker can do is show patterns over time. [Kip](/) records and scores your snoring loudness and frequency on your iPhone, so you can see trends across a week and bring real notes to your GP. It is a wellness tool, not a medical device, and it never measures blood oxygen or AHI.

If the snoring comes with breathing pauses, gasping or daytime exhaustion, that is worth a conversation. ## FAQ

### What is a normal AHI?

An AHI below 5 events per hour is considered normal. From 5 to 14 is graded mild, 15 to 30 is moderate, and over 30 is severe. These bands come from a clinical sleep study, not from a home gadget or app.

### Can a snoring app measure my AHI?

No. AHI needs measurements of airflow and blood oxygen that a phone cannot take from sound. Apps can track snoring loudness and frequency, which may flag patterns consistent with disrupted sleep, but they cannot produce an AHI. Only a clinical sleep study can.

### Is a high AHI dangerous?

A higher AHI means more frequent breathing events, and untreated sleep apnoea is linked to problems like high blood pressure over time. The figure itself is not a verdict you should act on alone. If you have been given an AHI or suspect sleep apnoea, discuss it with your GP, who can arrange the right assessment and care.
