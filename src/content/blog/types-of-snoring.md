Snoring is the sound of air moving through a narrowed airway and making relaxed tissue vibrate as you sleep. Where that narrowing happens changes both the sound you make and the things likely to help. Working out which of the types of snoring fits you is a useful first step.

## What are the main types of snoring?

There is no single official list, but most snoring falls into four overlapping groups based on the site of the obstruction. Many people are a mix of two.

| Type | Where it narrows | Typical clue | What tends to help |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Nasal | Inside the nose | Worse with a cold or congestion, snorting sound | Treat the blocked nose, [nasal strips](/blog/do-nasal-strips-work-for-snoring/) for some |
| Palatal | Soft palate and uvula | Low, fluttering, classic snore | Side sleeping, weight and alcohol changes |
| Tongue-base | Tongue falls backwards | Loudest on your back, higher and noisier | [Positional therapy](/blog/positional-therapy-for-snoring/), [mouthpieces](/blog/anti-snoring-mouthpieces-mad-guide/) |
| Mouth | Open-mouthed breathing | Dry mouth, breathing through the mouth | Address nasal blockage, mouth taping for some |

## What does each type sound like?

The soft palate and uvula are the most common things that vibrate, and they produce a low-pitched, fluttering palatal snore. Tongue-base snoring sits at higher frequencies and is noisier and harsher. Nasal snoring often comes with a snorting or whistling quality and tracks your congestion. [Mouth snoring](/blog/mouth-breathing-snoring/) usually leaves you with a dry mouth and a sore throat in the morning. The sound is a clue, not a verdict, but it helps you guess where to start.

## Does the type tell me the cause?

It points you in the right direction. Snoring is loudest and most frequent when you sleep on your back, because gravity pulls the tongue and soft palate backwards, which is why so many people are tongue-base or palatal snorers when supine. A blocked nose makes you three times more likely to be a habitual snorer, which suggests the nasal type. Knowing your likely type helps you pick a sensible first fix rather than guessing. For the full picture of why people snore, see [what causes snoring](/blog/what-causes-snoring/).

## How do I find out which type I am?

A few simple checks help. Notice whether snoring is worse on your back, whether your nose is blocked, and whether you wake with a dry mouth. Tracking a few nights of recordings gives you something concrete to compare, rather than relying on memory or a half-asleep partner. [Kip](/) records and scores your snoring on your iPhone, with nothing leaving the device, so you can see whether a change like sleeping on your side actually quietens things down. Loudness rises with sleep apnoea severity on average, but loud snoring alone never confirms it. If you notice breathing pauses, gasping or choking, [see your GP](/blog/when-to-see-gp-about-snoring/).

## FAQ

### Can the type of snoring change from night to night?

Yes. Congestion from a cold can turn a quiet sleeper into a nasal snorer, and alcohol or sleeping on your back can bring on louder palatal or tongue-base snoring. Many people are a mix of types, and which one dominates can shift with your position, your health and how much you have had to drink.

### Is one type of snoring more serious than another?

Not really. The type points to the likely cause and fix, but it does not grade how serious the snoring is. What matters more is whether snoring comes with breathing pauses, gasping or strong daytime tiredness. Those patterns, not the sound type, are the ones worth raising with a GP.

### Which type responds best to side sleeping?

Tongue-base and palatal snoring tend to improve most when you move off your back, because side sleeping stops the tongue and soft palate falling backwards. Studies show non-apnoeic snorers significantly reduce both snoring time and loudness on their side. Nasal snoring is less affected by position, so it usually needs the blocked nose dealt with instead.
