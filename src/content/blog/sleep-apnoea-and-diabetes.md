Sleep and blood sugar are more connected than most people realise. If you have been reading about metabolic health, you may have come across the link between sleep apnoea and diabetes. Here is what the evidence actually shows, kept plain and free of scare tactics.

## Does sleep apnoea raise the risk of type 2 diabetes?

The NHS lists type 2 diabetes among the conditions that untreated obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) can contribute to over time. The research behind this is fairly consistent. Repeated dips in breathing through the night disturb sleep and oxygen levels, and that pattern is associated with how the body handles blood sugar.

It is worth being precise here. These are findings across large groups of people, not a verdict on any one person. Plenty of people with OSA never develop diabetes, and many with diabetes have no sleep apnoea at all.

## How strong is the link? The dose-response evidence

The clearest picture comes from a dose-response meta-analysis. "Dose-response" simply means the risk climbs as the sleep apnoea gets more severe. Severity is measured clinically by the apnoea-hypopnoea index (AHI), the number of breathing interruptions per hour of sleep.

| Sleep apnoea severity | Associated risk of type 2 diabetes |
| --- | --- |
| Moderate | About 1.35 times higher |
| Moderate to severe | About 2.14 times higher |
| Severe | About 2.19 times higher |

So at moderate to severe levels, the risk of type 2 diabetes is roughly doubled compared with people without OSA. The pattern holds as severity rises, which is part of why researchers take the link seriously.

## Why this matters if you snore

Loud, heavy snoring can be a sign of OSA, although snoring on its own is not the same thing and does not confirm anything. Sleep apnoea is also very common and often goes unnoticed: an estimated 1.5 million UK adults have it, and most are undiagnosed.

## FAQ

### Can sleep apnoea cause type 2 diabetes?

Research shows a strong association between moderate to severe sleep apnoea and type 2 diabetes, with the risk roughly doubling at higher severity. Association is not the same as direct cause, and many factors are involved. It is a reason to look after your sleep and to raise concerns with your GP.

### Does treating sleep apnoea help blood sugar?

Treatment such as CPAP is decided by a clinician after a proper assessment, and effects on blood sugar can vary between people. The first step is getting any sleep apnoea recognised. Only a sleep study can diagnose it, so speak to your GP about referral.

### Can a snoring app tell me if I have diabetes risk?

No. A snoring app cannot diagnose sleep apnoea, diabetes, or any health risk. Kip tracks snoring loudness and frequency on your phone so you can spot trends and have a more informed conversation with your GP, who can arrange proper testing if needed.
