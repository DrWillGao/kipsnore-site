If you have high blood pressure and you also snore, it is fair to wonder whether the two are connected. The honest answer about snoring and high blood pressure is that snoring itself is not the problem. The thing worth understanding is what loud, heavy snoring can sometimes point to, and why that matters for your blood pressure.

## Does snoring and high blood pressure go together?

Plain snoring is the sound of soft tissue vibrating as you breathe. By itself it does not push your blood pressure up.

The link comes through obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA), where the airway narrows or closes again and again during sleep, briefly interrupting breathing. Each pause nudges the body's stress response and oxygen levels, and over time this is associated with higher blood pressure. Loud or heavy snoring is one of the common signs of OSA, which is why snoring and blood pressure get mentioned in the same breath. Snoring is the flag, not the cause.

## What does the research show?

The clearest evidence comes from the Wisconsin Sleep Cohort, which followed adults over four years. It found a dose-response link: the more breathing events people had at the start, the more likely they were to develop new high blood pressure.

| Sleep-disordered breathing at baseline | Odds of new high blood pressure over 4 years |
| --- | --- |
| None | Reference (1.0) |
| Mild (AHI 5 to 14.9) | About 2 times higher |
| Moderate or worse (AHI 15 or more) | About 2.9 times higher |

A few things to hold in mind. The "AHI" is the apnoea-hypopnoea index, a count of breathing events per hour that only a clinical sleep study can measure. These figures describe whole groups of people, not any one person. And no app or home gadget can produce an AHI or tell you that you have OSA.

## What does the NHS say?

The NHS lists high blood pressure among the problems that untreated sleep apnoea can lead to, alongside a higher chance of stroke, type 2 diabetes and heart disease. It frames sleep apnoea as something to get assessed rather than ignore.

The reassuring part: snoring is very common and usually not caused by anything serious. The NHS suggests seeing a GP if your snoring is severe or affects your or your partner's sleep, and contacting a doctor if breathing stops and starts, or there are gasping or choking noises. Those breathing-pause signs, not the snoring volume on its own, are what point towards apnoea. ## When should I see a GP?

Raise it with your GP if loud snoring comes with any of these:

- Breathing pauses, gasping or choking that a partner has noticed
- Waking up unrefreshed, with morning headaches
- Strong daytime sleepiness
- High blood pressure that is hard to control

These are the [sleep apnoea symptoms](/blog/sleep-apnoea-symptoms/) worth mentioning. Walking in with a few nights of recorded data helps. An app like [Kip](/) tracks how loud and how often you snore over time, all on your iPhone, so you have something concrete to show. Kip is a wellness and screening tool. It flags patterns consistent with disrupted sleep. It does not measure blood oxygen and it cannot diagnose anything.

## FAQ

### Can snoring raise my blood pressure?

Snoring on its own is not known to raise blood pressure. The concern is that loud, heavy snoring can be a sign of sleep apnoea, and untreated apnoea is linked to higher blood pressure in large studies. If you snore and have high blood pressure, it is worth a GP chat.

### Will treating sleep apnoea lower my blood pressure?

Treating apnoea is part of managing the conditions linked to it, and your GP or a sleep clinic can advise on what is right for you. Only a sleep study can diagnose apnoea and decide on treatment, so this is a conversation to have with a doctor rather than something to judge at home.

### Can an app tell me if my snoring is affecting my blood pressure?

No. No app can diagnose sleep apnoea or measure your blood pressure during sleep. A snore tracker can show you how loud and how often you snore over time, which is useful information to bring to your GP, but it is not a medical test and it does not diagnose anything.
