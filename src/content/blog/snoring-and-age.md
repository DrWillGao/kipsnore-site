Many people find their snoring creeps up over the years, and it is not your imagination. The link between snoring and age is real: the airway changes as we get older, and the numbers back this up. Here is what the research actually shows, and why subtle signs in later life are worth paying attention to.

## How are snoring and age linked?

For most people, snoring does increase with age, up to a point. The chance of having obstructive sleep apnoea (the condition where breathing keeps pausing in sleep) climbs steadily with age and then plateaus around 65. After that, it does not keep rising in the same way. One study found that among adults over 65 assessed for sleep apnoea, about 83 percent had an apnoea-hypopnoea index above 5, and roughly half were above 15. That is far higher than in middle age.

The airway tissues relax and lose tone with age, so they vibrate and narrow more easily. Weight changes through the years play a part too. You can read more about the wider picture in our guide on [snoring risk factors](/blog/snoring-risk-factors/).

## Why do older adults notice fewer symptoms?

This is the part that surprises people. Older adults often report milder symptoms even when their night-time event rate is higher. In the same over-65 group, sleepiness scores were actually lower than in younger patients, despite more pauses in breathing. So the daytime warning signs that usually prompt a doctor's visit can be muted.

That means the usual cues, like feeling exhausted or being told you stopped breathing, may be quieter. Loud snoring is reported by about a third of older men and a fifth of older women, and it tends to drop off after 75. Quieter does not always mean better, so subtle changes matter.

## What signs should an older person watch for?

Because symptoms can be soft, it helps to know what to look out for:

| Sign | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Snoring that has got louder or more frequent | A common pattern as the airway changes |
| Pauses, gasping or choking sounds in sleep | A partner often notices these first |
| Waking unrefreshed despite enough hours | Sleep may be fragmented, not restful |
| New morning headaches or low mood | Can accompany disrupted breathing |

If breathing seems to stop and start, or there is gasping or choking, that is worth raising with a GP. The NHS notes sleep apnoea can be serious if it is not treated. Our piece on [when to see your GP about snoring](/blog/when-to-see-gp-about-snoring/) walks through what to expect.

## FAQ

### At what age does snoring usually peak?

Risk tends to rise until around 65 and then plateaus. The prevalence of sleep apnoea does not keep climbing steeply after that, although loud snoring itself can ease a little after 75. The exact pattern varies from person to person.

### Is it normal to start snoring in your 60s?

It is common, because airway tissues relax more with age and breathing can become more disrupted in sleep. Common does not mean it should be ignored, though. If new snoring comes with gasping, pauses or daytime tiredness, mention it to your GP.

### Why am I tired even though I sleep enough hours?

Disrupted breathing can fragment sleep without fully waking you, so the hours look fine but the rest is poor. In older adults this can happen with few obvious symptoms. If it persists, it is worth a conversation with your GP rather than just blaming age.
