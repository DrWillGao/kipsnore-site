If a partner has told you that you snore, and your weight has crept up over the years, the two may be linked. Of all the things that make snoring worse, body weight is the one with the strongest evidence behind it. The good news is that it is also one of the few you can change.

## Why does weight make snoring worse?

Snoring happens when air pushes through a narrowed airway and makes relaxed tissue at the back of the throat vibrate. Carrying extra weight, especially around the neck and upper body, adds soft tissue that crowds that space. A larger neck circumference, often flagged above 40 cm (about 16 inches) in screening tools, presses on the airway from outside. There is less room for air to pass, so the vibration gets louder and more frequent.

The same crowding is the leading cause of obstructive sleep apnoea, where the airway narrows so far that breathing briefly pauses. More than half of all sleep apnoea cases are linked to excess body weight, and the odds of apnoea rise about 14 percent for each one-point rise in body mass index. Weight is one of several [snoring risk factors](/blog/snoring-risk-factors/), and it sits alongside [alcohol](/blog/snoring-and-alcohol/) and back-sleeping in making things worse.

## Does losing weight stop snoring?

Weight loss is the best-evidenced lifestyle change for snoring and apnoea. In the Sleep AHEAD trial, people who lost around 10.8 kg cut their apnoea events by roughly 10 more per hour than the control group. Across studies, losing 10 to 15 percent of your body weight can halve the number of apnoea events.

Here is how modest changes compare:

| Change | What the evidence suggests |
| --- | --- |
| 10 to 15 percent weight loss | Can roughly halve sleep apnoea events |
| About 10 kg loss (Sleep AHEAD) | Cut apnoea events by about 10 per hour |
| 10 percent weight gain | Raises odds of moderate-or-worse apnoea about six-fold |

The arrow points both ways. Weight gain makes things worse as reliably as loss makes them better, so even holding steady helps. If you want to know whether a change is actually working, tracking your snoring over a few weeks with an app like [Kip](/) gives you a trend rather than a guess. For more on lifestyle steps, see [how to stop snoring](/blog/how-to-stop-snoring/).

## When should weight-related snoring worry me?

Snoring on its own is usually nothing serious. It is the company it keeps that matters. See your GP if loud snoring comes with breathing pauses, gasping or choking that a partner notices, or strong daytime tiredness. These can be signs of sleep apnoea, which only a clinical sleep study can diagnose. An app cannot. Raising it early is sensible, not alarmist.

## FAQ

### How much weight do I need to lose to stop snoring?

There is no single number, but the evidence points to meaningful change with a 10 to 15 percent loss, which can roughly halve sleep apnoea events. Even modest loss can reduce snoring and the pressure on your airway. Smaller, steady changes are easier to keep than crash diets.

### Can thin people still snore?

Yes. Weight is the strongest changeable cause, but not the only one. Anatomy, a blocked nose, alcohol, smoking, age and back-sleeping all play a part, so slim people snore too. Weight loss simply removes one of the biggest and most modifiable factors.

### Does where I carry weight matter for snoring?

Weight around the neck and upper body matters most, because that is the tissue crowding the airway. A larger neck size is an independent risk factor used in sleep apnoea screening. Two people at the same weight can snore very differently depending on their build.
