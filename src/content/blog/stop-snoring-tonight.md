If you are searching for how to stop snoring tonight, the fixes below take minutes. None of them treat a medical condition, so if your snoring is loud and persistent, treat tonight as a stopgap and read the caveat at the end.

## How to stop snoring tonight: 7 quick fixes

Work through these in order. The first two help most people the most.

1. **Roll onto your side.** Snoring is loudest on your back, because gravity pulls the tongue and soft palate backwards. A tennis ball in a sock, pinned to the back of your pyjama top, stops you rolling over.
2. **Skip the nightcap.** Alcohol relaxes the muscles that hold your airway open, so a late drink can make snoring worse, especially if you already snore. Leave a few hours before bed.
3. **Clear a blocked nose.** Congestion pushes you towards mouth breathing, which encourages snoring. A saline rinse, a hot shower, or nasal strips can help. See our guide to a [blocked nose and snoring](/blog/blocked-nose-snoring/).
4. **Prop your head up.** Raising the head of the bed a little, or a firmer pillow, can ease airflow. Too many pillows kink the neck, so keep it gentle.
5. **Stay hydrated.** Dry airways can make snoring noisier. Sip water through the day, not right before bed.
6. **Avoid sedatives before bed.** Sleeping pills act on the airway muscles much like alcohol. The NHS advises not using them unless a doctor has recommended them.
7. **Keep the air moist.** Dry, heated air irritates the throat and nose. A humidifier, or a damp towel on the radiator, can help.

For why these work, see [what causes snoring](/blog/what-causes-snoring/) and our main [how to stop snoring](/blog/how-to-stop-snoring/) guide.

## Do these quick fixes actually work?

They can quieten a single night, especially the position and alcohol changes. What they cannot do is fix the underlying habit. The honest test is whether a change holds up over a week, not one lucky night. ## When should I see a GP about snoring?

Quick fixes are fine for the occasional noisy night. But see your GP if your snoring is loud most nights, disturbs your or your partner's sleep, or comes with breathing pauses, gasping, or daytime tiredness. Those patterns can be consistent with disrupted sleep, and only a clinical sleep study can diagnose sleep apnoea. This is reassurance, not alarm: snoring is very common and usually not serious.

## FAQ

### Does sleeping on your side really stop snoring?

For many people, yes, at least partly. Studies show snoring is loudest and most frequent when you lie on your back, because the tongue and soft palate fall backwards and narrow the airway. Switching to your side often reduces both how often and how loudly you snore, though it may not stop it completely.

### Why does alcohol make snoring worse?

Alcohol relaxes the muscles in your upper airway, so the soft tissue is more likely to collapse and vibrate as you breathe. A meta-analysis found alcohol worsened breathing during sleep, with a larger effect in people who already snore. Leaving a gap before bed helps.

### What if I still snore every night after trying these?

Quick fixes manage the occasional noisy night, not a persistent habit. If you snore loudly most nights, or your partner notices breathing pauses, gasping, or you feel tired through the day, book a GP appointment. A few nights of tracked data can make that conversation more useful.
