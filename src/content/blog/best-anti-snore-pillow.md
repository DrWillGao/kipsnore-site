An anti snore pillow is sold on a simple promise: change how your head sits and you snore less. There is a real mechanism behind that, but the marketing tends to run ahead of the evidence. Here is what these pillows do, who they help, and how to check if one works for you.

## How does an anti snore pillow work?

Most work in one of two ways, and the better ones do both.

- **Encouraging side sleeping.** Snoring is loudest and most frequent when you sleep on your back, because gravity pulls the tongue and soft palate backwards and narrows the airway. In one study, snorers cut both their snoring time and its loudness simply by switching to their side. Wedge and contoured pillows try to make the back position less comfortable so you settle on your side.
- **Aligning the head and neck.** A pillow at the right height keeps the neck neutral rather than letting the chin drop towards the chest, which can pinch the airway. Better alignment means smoother airflow and less tissue vibration.

So the honest version is this: an anti snore pillow is really a [positional therapy](/blog/positional-therapy-for-snoring/) tool with a comfortable shape. The position is doing most of the work, not the foam.

## Who do anti snore pillows help most?

They tend to help people whose snoring is clearly worse on their back and milder on their side. If your snoring barely changes with position, a pillow is less likely to do much. They are one of several gentle options worth trying, alongside the wider list in [how to stop snoring](/blog/how-to-stop-snoring/) and the [best sleeping position to stop snoring](/blog/best-sleeping-position-to-stop-snoring/).

A pillow will not help if the main problem is a blocked nose, weight, alcohol before bed, or sleep apnoea. Those need their own approach.

## How do I know if a pillow actually reduces my snoring?

Buy one thing, change nothing else, and measure. The fairest test is a few nights of baseline followed by a few nights with the pillow.

| Step | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| 1. Baseline | Record 3 to 4 normal nights first. |
| 2. Switch | Add the pillow and keep everything else the same. |
| 3. Compare | Look at snore loudness and time, not just one night. |

An app like [Kip](/) makes this easy. It records and scores your snoring on the iPhone itself, with no cloud upload, so you can see whether the numbers genuinely move. If they do, keep the pillow. If they do not, you have saved yourself from guessing.

## When should I see my GP?

A pillow is a comfort experiment, not a treatment. If you snore loudly and your partner notices you stop breathing, gasp or choke, or you feel exhausted despite a full night, see your GP. Those patterns can be consistent with disrupted sleep, and only a clinical sleep study can diagnose sleep apnoea.

## FAQ

### Are anti snore pillows worth the money?

They can be, if your snoring is clearly worse on your back. For a side-dominant snorer, a pillow that keeps you off your back is a cheap, low-risk thing to try. The only way to know is to record a baseline and compare, rather than rely on how you feel.

### Can a pillow cure snoring?

No. A pillow can reduce snoring for some people by improving position and alignment, but it does not cure it. If snoring continues or comes with breathing pauses or daytime tiredness, speak to your GP.

### Do anti snore pillows help with sleep apnoea?

They are not a treatment for sleep apnoea. Position can affect how often breathing is interrupted, but a pillow is no substitute for proper assessment. If you suspect apnoea, see your GP or a sleep clinic, as only a clinical sleep study can diagnose it.
