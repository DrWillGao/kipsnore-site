If you snore, an anti snoring mouthpiece is one of the more practical things you can try. They are widely sold, they work for many people, and a dentist can fit a proper one. Here is how they work and what to weigh up before buying.

## How does an anti snoring mouthpiece work?

Snoring happens when relaxed soft tissue in the throat vibrates as you breathe. A mandibular advancement device clips over your upper and lower teeth and gently pushes the lower jaw forward. That movement pulls the tongue base forward too, opens the airway, and gives the air a smoother path. Less narrowing means less vibration, and quieter nights.

MADs are most useful for snoring and for mild to moderate obstructive sleep apnoea, and they are the main alternative to CPAP. For moderate to severe cases, CPAP is the first-line treatment and is more effective. Only a clinical sleep study can confirm sleep apnoea, so see your GP first.

## Boil-and-bite or custom from a dentist?

There are two main routes, and they trade cost against fit.

| Type | How you get it | Pros | Things to know |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Boil-and-bite | Off the shelf, moulded at home in hot water | Cheap, available today | Bulkier fit, may not last, less precise |
| Custom-made | Dentist takes an impression | Better fit, more durable, jaw set accurately | Costs more, takes a couple of visits |

A dentist can also check your teeth and gums are healthy enough for a device, which the home kits cannot do.

## What are the side effects?

Most are mild and settle within a week or two. They can include:

- Jaw or tooth ache in the morning
- More saliva, or a dry mouth
- Tender gums
- Small shifts in your bite over long-term use

If pain persists or your bite feels off, stop and speak to your dentist. People with loose teeth, gum disease or jaw joint problems should get professional advice before trying one.

## FAQ

### Are anti-snoring mouthpieces safe to use every night?

For most people, yes, once the fit is comfortable. A custom device made by a dentist is the safer long-term choice because the jaw position is set correctly and your teeth are checked first. Stop and seek advice if you get lasting jaw pain or bite changes.

### Do mouthpieces help with sleep apnoea?

They can help with mild to moderate obstructive sleep apnoea, and oral devices are the main alternative to CPAP. For moderate to severe cases, CPAP is more effective and is the first-line treatment. Only a clinical sleep study can confirm apnoea, so see your GP first.

### How do I know if my mouthpiece is working?

Ask your partner, and measure it. Track how loud and how often you snore for a few nights before you start, then again with the device in. A clear drop in your snoring score and frequency is the practical sign it is helping.
