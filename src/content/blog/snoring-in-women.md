The picture most people have of sleep apnoea is a middle-aged man snoring loudly and gasping in his sleep. That picture leaves a lot of women out. Sleep apnoea in women is more easily missed, but it is far from rare, and it tends to show up in quieter ways.

## Why is sleep apnoea missed in women?

Obstructive sleep apnoea (OSA) is roughly two to three times more common in men than women, so it gets thought of as a male condition. The bigger problem is how it presents. Women more often report fatigue, insomnia, morning headaches and mood changes rather than loud snoring and witnessed breathing pauses. Their sleep studies also tend to show lower event counts. So the classic warning signs are less obvious, and the condition is more easily put down to stress, low iron or just being busy.

## What symptoms should women look out for?

The signs are real, they are just easier to overlook. Worth raising with a GP:

- Waking tired most mornings, even after a full night in bed
- Trouble falling or staying asleep (insomnia rather than obvious snoring)
- Morning headaches
- Low mood, irritability or feeling foggy and forgetful
- Snoring, especially if it is new or getting louder

If a few of these sound familiar, it is worth a conversation. This is not about alarming you. These are simply the patterns a doctor wants to hear about. You can read more in our guide to [sleep apnoea symptoms](/blog/sleep-apnoea-symptoms/) and to [feeling tired after a full night's sleep](/blog/tired-after-full-night-sleep/).

## Does menopause increase the risk?

Yes. Risk climbs noticeably around menopause. In the Wisconsin Sleep Cohort, postmenopausal women had about 2.6 times the odds of sleep-disordered breathing compared with premenopausal women, independent of age and body weight. Falling oestrogen and progesterone are thought to play a part: lower levels are linked to more snoring and more irregular breathing during sleep.

## FAQ

### Can women have sleep apnoea without loud snoring?

Yes. Women often present with fatigue, insomnia, morning headaches and low mood rather than the loud snoring and witnessed pauses seen more typically in men. Because the signs are quieter, the condition is more easily missed. If you wake unrefreshed most days, it is worth raising with your GP.

### Why does menopause raise the risk of sleep apnoea?

Risk rises around menopause as oestrogen and progesterone levels fall. One large study found postmenopausal women had roughly 2.6 times the odds of sleep-disordered breathing versus premenopausal women, independent of age and weight. New or worsening snoring after menopause is worth a GP conversation.

### Can an app diagnose sleep apnoea in women?

No. No app can diagnose sleep apnoea. Only a sleep study can. An app like Kip is a wellness and screening tool that tracks how loud and frequent your snoring is, so you can have an informed conversation with your GP and get referred to a sleep clinic if needed.
