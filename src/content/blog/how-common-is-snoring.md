If your partner has nudged you about your snoring, you are in large company. So how common is snoring? It is one of the most common things adults do in their sleep, and most of the time it is nothing serious. The figures below put it in context and show why a small number of people are worth a closer look.

## How common is snoring in the UK?

A lot of people snore. A UK Biobank analysis of around 408,000 people put habitual snoring at roughly 36 percent of adults. It is more common in men (35 to 45 percent) than in women (15 to 28 percent), and a separate UK survey found 38 percent of men and 30 percent of women snore. The NHS describes snoring as "very common and not usually caused by anything serious".

So if you snore, you are firmly in the majority. For most people it is a noise problem, not a health one. If you want to understand the mechanics, see [what causes snoring](/blog/what-causes-snoring/).

## How common is sleep apnoea?

Less common than snoring, but more common than the diagnosis figures suggest. Here is the UK picture:

| Group | Estimate |
| --- | --- |
| UK adults with obstructive sleep apnoea | About 1.5 million |
| Receiving treatment | Around 330,000 |
| Estimated undiagnosed | Up to roughly 85 percent |

In other words, most people with apnoea do not yet know they have it. That gap is the reason it is worth paying attention to your own patterns, especially if loud snoring comes with daytime tiredness. The difference between the two is covered in [snoring versus sleep apnoea](/blog/snoring-vs-sleep-apnoea/).

## What do these numbers mean for me?

They are reassuring and motivating at the same time. Snoring on its own is usual and rarely a problem. But loud snoring alongside breathing pauses, gasping, or waking up tired is the pattern worth raising with a GP, because apnoea is so often missed.

Tracking a few nights with an app like [Kip](/) gives you real data on how loud and how often you snore, on the iPhone, with nothing leaving the device. It cannot diagnose anything, but it turns a vague worry into something concrete to discuss. If the signs in [sleep apnoea symptoms](/blog/sleep-apnoea-symptoms/) sound familiar, see your GP.

## FAQ

### Is it normal to snore every night?

Yes, snoring most nights is common and usually harmless. Around 36 percent of UK adults snore habitually. It becomes worth checking if it is loud and comes with breathing pauses, gasping, or persistent daytime tiredness, in which case see your GP.

### How many people have undiagnosed sleep apnoea?

In the UK, an estimated 1.5 million adults have obstructive sleep apnoea, and up to roughly 85 percent of cases are thought to be undiagnosed. Only about 330,000 people are receiving treatment, which is why noticing your own patterns matters.

### Does snoring mean I have sleep apnoea?

No. Snoring alone is not a diagnosis. Plenty of people snore loudly without apnoea. Apnoea is graded by breathing pauses during sleep, something only a clinical sleep study can measure. An app cannot diagnose it, but tracking can help you decide whether to talk to your GP.
