If your partner snoring is keeping you awake, you are not being precious. In one survey of 1,000 people whose partner snores at least weekly, 75 percent said it affected their sleep and 44 percent felt sleepy the next day. Here is what helps quickly, and what fixes it for good.

## What can I do tonight to block out snoring?

These are the stop-gaps. They protect your sleep while you and your partner sort out the cause.

| Tactic | Why it helps |
| --- | --- |
| Foam or wax earplugs | Cuts the peak loudness, which often sits around 50 to 65 dB |
| White-noise or a fan | Masks the rhythm so your brain stops latching onto it |
| Go to bed 20 minutes first | You fall asleep before the noise starts |
| Nudge them onto their side | Snoring is loudest on the back, when the tongue falls backwards |

None of these treat the snoring. They buy you rest while you do.

## Does treating my partner's snoring help me sleep?

Yes, and the effect is measured. In one study of couples, the partner's sleep efficiency rose from 74 to 87 percent once the snorer was treated, recovering around an hour of sleep a night. So the kindest thing for both of you is to deal with the cause, not just the symptom. Start with the simple changes in [how to stop snoring](/blog/how-to-stop-snoring/), like side-sleeping, less alcohol before bed, and clearing a blocked nose.

## How do I raise it without a row?

Lead with how tired you both are, not with blame. It helps to bring something concrete rather than "you kept me up again". A few nights of recorded data turns a vague complaint into a shared problem to solve. [Kip](/) records and scores snoring on the iPhone itself, with no audio leaving the phone, so you can both see how loud it actually gets and whether a change like side-sleeping helps. It is free for three nights. See [how to track snoring](/blog/how-to-track-snoring/) for the basics.

## When should we see a GP?

Loud snoring on its own is usually harmless. Book a GP appointment if your partner's snoring comes with pauses in breathing, or gasping and choking sounds, especially alongside daytime sleepiness. Those can point to patterns consistent with disrupted sleep that only a clinical sleep study can assess. Our guide on [when to see a GP about snoring](/blog/when-to-see-gp-about-snoring/) walks through the signs.

## FAQ

### Is it normal to sleep in a separate room because of snoring?

It is common and nothing to feel guilty about. Many couples use a "sleep divorce" to protect rest while they work on the cause. Treating the snoring often makes it unnecessary, so see it as a temporary fix rather than a permanent one.

### Can I make my partner stop snoring without them noticing?

Gently nudging them onto their side often softens snoring on the spot, since it is loudest when they lie on their back. For a lasting change, though, they need to be part of it. Losing the back-sleeping habit and cutting late-night alcohol both help.

### How loud is snoring, really?

Average snoring sits around 50 to 65 dB, similar to a conversation. Heavy snoring can reach about 90 dB, comparable to a lawnmower. That is more than enough to keep a light sleeper awake, which is why your tiredness is real and worth acting on.
