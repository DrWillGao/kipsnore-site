Plenty of people snore only when they have a cold or a flare of hay fever. That is not a coincidence. A stuffy nose changes the way air moves through your airway, and the result is more noise at night.

## Why does a blocked nose make you snore?

Snoring is the sound of relaxed soft tissues vibrating as you breathe in your sleep. The NHS describes it as your tongue, mouth, throat or the airways in your nose vibrating as these parts relax and narrow.

When your nose is blocked, two things happen. Air has to squeeze through a narrower channel, so it speeds up and becomes turbulent, which makes tissues flutter more. And a blocked nose pushes you towards breathing through your mouth, which drops the jaw back and narrows the throat. Both raise the odds of a noisy night. People with nasal congestion are around three times more likely to be habitual snorers, and nasal symptoms and snoring feed into each other over time.

If you only snore with an open mouth, our guide to [mouth breathing and snoring](/blog/mouth-breathing-snoring/) digs into that pattern.

## What causes the congestion in the first place?

Common culprits include:

- A cold or other short-lived infection
- Allergic rhinitis, such as hay fever or a reaction to dust or pets
- Non-allergic rhinitis, where the lining stays inflamed without an obvious trigger
- A deviated septum or nasal polyps narrowing one or both sides
- Smoking, which inflames the nasal lining and raises nasal resistance

Working out which one applies matters, because the fix differs. For the bigger picture of what sets off snoring, see [what causes snoring](/blog/what-causes-snoring/).

## What actually helps clear it?

| Approach | What it does |
| --- | --- |
| Saline rinse or spray | Flushes mucus and allergens, drug-free, safe nightly |
| Steroid nasal spray | Calms inflammation from rhinitis over days to weeks |
| Nasal strips or dilators | Hold the nostrils open mechanically |
| Treating allergies | Antihistamines and avoiding triggers reduce swelling |

For rhinitis, a steroid nasal spray has decent evidence: in one trial, fluticasone lowered snoring-related breathing measures compared with placebo. These sprays take a little while to work, so give them a fair run rather than a single night. [Nasal strips](/blog/do-nasal-strips-work-for-snoring/) can help if your problem is the nostrils collapsing, though they do nothing for the throat.

## When should I see a GP?

Snoring is very common and usually nothing serious. But see your GP if it is affecting your sleep or your partner's, or if congestion never clears. Contact a doctor if anyone notices you stop breathing, gasp or choke in your sleep, as these can be signs of something that needs assessment. An app cannot diagnose any condition, and only a clinical sleep study can.

## FAQ

### Will treating my hay fever stop my snoring?
It can reduce it. Allergic rhinitis swells the nasal lining, and people with congestion snore far more often, so calming the allergy with antihistamines, a steroid spray or trigger avoidance often quietens the night. It will not help snoring that comes from the throat or tongue base.

### Do nasal strips work for a blocked nose?
They help if your nostrils tend to collapse inwards, by gently holding them open. They do nothing for swelling deeper in the nose or for throat-based snoring, so pair them with a saline rinse or steroid spray if congestion is the main issue.

### Why is my blocked nose worse lying down at night?
Lying flat increases blood flow to the nasal lining, so it swells and feels more blocked, and gravity no longer helps mucus drain. This is normal. A saline rinse before bed and slightly raising the head of the bed can ease it.
