If a partner has nudged you about your snoring and you smoke, the two are probably connected. So does smoking cause snoring? In short, yes. Smoking is one of the modifiable risk factors the NHS lists for snoring, alongside weight, alcohol and sleeping on your back. The encouraging part is that quitting can help on both counts.

## How does smoking cause snoring?

Snoring happens when air moves through a narrowed airway and makes the relaxed tissue at the back of your throat vibrate. Anything that swells or crowds that airway makes the sound more likely and often louder.

Tobacco smoke irritates and inflames the lining of the nose and throat. That swelling raises nasal resistance and narrows the space air has to pass through. A blocked, congested nose pushes you towards mouth breathing, which itself encourages snoring. So smoking does not just nudge one part of the chain. It adds inflammation, congestion and narrowing all at once.

## Do smokers have more sleep apnoea?

The risk is not limited to noise. A large review found that, compared with people who have never smoked, the odds of obstructive sleep apnoea were higher in both ex-smokers and current smokers, and heavy smokers (more than 20 pack-years) carried the most risk.

| Smoking status | Odds of OSA vs never-smokers |
| --- | --- |
| Ex-smoker | About 1.5 times |
| Current smoker | About 1.8 times |

These are population-level associations, not a personal verdict. Snoring and apnoea overlap but are not the same thing, and only a clinical sleep study can diagnose apnoea. If your snoring comes with breathing pauses, gasping or daytime exhaustion, see your GP. Our guide on [snoring versus sleep apnoea](/blog/snoring-vs-sleep-apnoea/) covers the warning signs.

## Does second-hand smoke matter too?

It does. A meta-analysis linked exposure to second-hand smoke with possible OSA (around 1.6 times the risk). So a smoke-filled home can affect a non-smoking partner's sleep as well. Clearing the air helps everyone in the bedroom.

## Will quitting smoking stop snoring?

Quitting removes the daily irritation, so the swelling in your nose and throat has a chance to settle. It is rarely the only factor, which is why it sits among the wider [risk factors for snoring](/blog/snoring-risk-factors/), but it is one of the few you can change for free. ## FAQ

### Does quitting smoking reduce snoring?

It can help. Stopping removes the daily irritation that inflames and swells the lining of your nose and throat, so the airway has a chance to open up. Snoring usually has more than one cause, so quitting may not stop it entirely, but it tackles a real and avoidable factor.

### Why do I snore more after smoking?

Tobacco smoke inflames the nose and throat, which swells the tissue and narrows the airway. A congested nose also pushes you towards breathing through your mouth, and that makes snoring more likely. The effect builds up the more and longer you smoke.

### Can vaping cause snoring too?

The strongest evidence is for tobacco smoke, which clearly inflames the airway. Vaping is newer and less studied, but any product that irritates the nose and throat could in theory have a similar effect. If snoring bothers you or your partner, it is worth raising with your GP.
